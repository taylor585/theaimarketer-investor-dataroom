1a

Start here — the four numbers behind everything

Every calculation in this document is built from four numbers. Each one comes from a real test we ran on the live system, with the cost recorded automatically — none of them are guesses. Two minutes here and every other tab reads easily.

$1.02 — the cost of one campaign

We give the system one client's details. About five minutes later it hands back a complete marketing campaign: customer research, 72 emails, ad copy, headlines, images, video scripts — 9 finished pieces. The AI bill for all of that, recorded automatically: $1.02. If we ask it to target three different customer types at once (19 pieces of content), the bill is $1.93 — not three times more, because the research is only done once.

1 campaign, 9 pieces of content = $1.02 1 campaign, 3 audiences, 19 pieces = $1.93

$0.29 — the cost of one lead

A "lead" is a real business that is actively looking to buy a service — the system finds them, collects their details, and grades them. In our live test we asked it to find 50 businesses looking for someone to run their Meta (Facebook) ads. It found all 50. The total AI bill was $14.46. Divide the bill by the leads:

$14.46 total ÷ 50 leads = $0.29 per lead

Twenty-nine cents to find one real, ready-to-buy business. Hold onto that number — it is why every guarantee in the Orange Questions tab is safe.

80+ — the quality score

The system grades every lead it finds from 0 to 100 on how ready they are to buy — are they asking for the service right now? Did they mention a budget? Did they say "urgent"? We only count and only sell leads that score 80 or above. Anything below 80 is thrown away and the system keeps hunting until it finds one that qualifies. So when this document says "a lead," it always means a lead scoring 80 or more.

$52.73 — the selling price of one lead

Each lead gets a sale price based on its score — hotter lead, higher price. Our set retail bands run from $50 for an 80-score lead up to $125 for a 95+ lead (Lead Hunter 5c). In the live test, the 64 leads we produced carried AI-suggested prices adding up to $3,375. Divide the total value by the number of leads:

$3,375 total value ÷ 64 leads = $52.73 average per lead

So whenever you see "$52.73 average" anywhere in this document, it means: the average selling price of one lead from the live test. A lead costs us $0.29 to make and sells for about $52.73 — that gap is the business.

In plain English — the whole business in one sentence
The machine makes two things extremely cheaply — finished marketing campaigns (about $1 each) and ready-to-buy sales leads (about 29 cents each) — and both of them sell for many times what they cost. Everything else in this document is just deciding who gets to use the machine and how they pay for it.
1b

Executive summary — what we're doing and why

What exists today. A working, tested system (10 tools, all live) that produces complete marketing campaigns for $1.02–$1.93 each and finds scored sales leads for $0.29 each. Every run's cost is recorded automatically, the output has been graded by independent reviewers (copy 8/10, honesty 9/10), and nothing goes to a client without a human pressing "approve" first. Full detail in The Proof.

The pivot. Right now, only we can use this system. The plan is to split it into two versions: (a) our own internal version, for running our clients, and (b) a version other businesses pay a monthly subscription to use — with their own login, their own client list, and strict limits on how much they can use. That second version is what the Republiq offer calls the "AI Ad Buyer." How the split works is in The Split.

Why the numbers work. If a customer pays $299 a month and uses every single drop of their allowance, our AI bill for them is about $19. We keep the rest. Most customers won't come close to their allowance — so real margins are higher still. The risk in this business isn't cost. It's making sure nobody can run up our bill — and Plans & Billing shows the three locks that make that impossible.

And the launch is mapped, not implied. Naming, the 7-day customer onboarding runbook, the sales team's demo script and objection answers, the training layer, and the go-live checklist are all written — in Go to Market. Every question Will asked on the calls has a one-line answer with a link, in Every Question. A green light tomorrow starts the launch clock, not another planning cycle.

The one-liner for the room: "The machine is already built and already measured — about a dollar per campaign, 29 cents per lead. What we're building over the next month isn't the product. It's the cash register: the accounts, the limits, and the billing that let us sell the machine without ever touching it."

In plain English
Remember
Will's exact worry on the call: "it's going to be on our billing account — how do we make sure we don't get cornered on usage if someone's going ham on it?"
What it is
Every customer's account works like a prepaid phone plan. Their plan comes with an allowance. When the allowance is used up, the system stops generating for them — politely, with an "upgrade or wait until next month" message. There is no way for a customer to keep spending our money, because the limit is enforced by the machine, not by us noticing.
Why it matters
This is the answer to the single biggest financial risk in the whole model. Our maximum possible loss per customer per month is a known, small number — the "worst-case AI bill" column above.

The three locks (all three run at once)

Lock 1 · A capped wallet per customer

Each customer runs on their own sub-key of our AI account with a hard dollar limit — like giving each customer a prepaid card instead of access to our bank account. When it's empty, generation stops. Our master bill physically cannot be run up by one customer.

Lock 2 · Allowance rules in the system

Campaign counts, the 3-re-runs rule and tool limits are checked by the server before every generation — the same checkpoint that runs the human approval gate today. Every use is written to the ledger, which is what the Sub-Account Manager displays.

Lock 3 · No pay, no play

Card payment fails → 72-hour grace period → the account pauses itself. It un-pauses automatically the moment payment goes through. And customers who prefer it can connect their own AI account instead — then their usage never touches our bill at all.

How this fits the Republiq offer: Venture customers ($2,499/month) get the top "Network" plan included — and its worst-possible cost to us is ~$151, so it fits inside their monthly with enormous room to spare. Operators can then resell access to their own customers at a suggested $199–499/month per seat, with Republiq taking a cut of each seat. After their 12-month term: license-to-own — they keep the system running on their own AI account and pay us a smaller maintenance fee. That's the "waterfall" from the call, with prices on it. Lead Hunter — three ways to make money from a 29-cent lead

Will proposed three business models on the call. They aren't rivals — they're three lanes of the same road, and we recommend running all three.

In plain English — the fishing version
The machine is a boat that catches fish for 29 cents each, and fish sell for about $52 each. We can: (1) catch fish and sell them ourselves, (2) rent the boat out for a monthly fee and let others fish, or (3) use the boat to find people who buy fish — which is how we back up the promises in Will's offer. Same boat, three incomes.

Lane 1 — We make the leads and sell them

~99% margin

How it works: we spend 29 cents to make a lead, sell it wholesale to Republiq operators, and they resell it at $50–125 depending on its score. The limit isn't cost — it's how many niches we can operate carefully at once. Verdict: switch on now. It generates cash immediately and stocks the "wholesale lead access" shelf in Will's offer.

Lane 2 — We rent out the machine

Monthly subscriptions

How it works: operators run the Lead Hunter themselves inside their subscription (with the limits in Plans & Billing), or unlimited on their own AI account. We carry zero delivery responsibility — a training video replaces our labour. Verdict: this is the core software business an investor is buying growth in, and the "license-to-own" story at month 12.

Lane 3 — We find the buyers

Backs the guarantees

How it works: the same engine that finds leads also finds businesses that buy leads — that's literally what the 50-lead test was (businesses wanting Meta ads help). This lane is what makes the promises in the Orange Questions tab safe to sign. Verdict: don't sell it separately — bake it into the offer's guarantees.

5b

The two kinds of leads — don't mix them up

On the call Will asked: "an opt-in lead would cost more, right — maybe $15–30 if we're running paid traffic?" Correct — because it's a different animal. The offer sells both, so everyone on the sales team needs this distinction cold.

Kind 1 · Intent-found lead — $0.29 measured

The AI finds a business that is publicly asking for the service right now — posting "my cost per lead is suffering, I need someone to take over my Meta ads." They haven't given anyone their details; we found them. Cost: $0.29 (measured, Start Here). They're hot, but someone still has to reach out — see "from contact to conversation" below.

Kind 2 · Opt-in lead — est. $15–30 estimate — being measured

A person fills in a form after seeing a paid ad — they've raised their hand and expect a call. Warmer, but dearer: the cost is real ad spend, and $15–30 is an industry estimate, not our measured number — the AI Ad Buyer is collecting the real figure on WealthWise now (that's why Orange Q4 is on HOLD). These are the leads a customer's own ad budget generates for their fulfilment calendar.

In plain English
Kind 1 is us finding people who are already shopping (29 cents, we knock on their door). Kind 2 is people coming to us off an ad (they knock, but each knock costs real ad money). The offer's wholesale lead supply and the guarantees run on Kind 1. The customer's own ad campaigns produce Kind 2. Different costs, different promises — never quote one's price for the other.

From contact to conversation — how a 29-cent name becomes a booked meeting

A list is not a pipeline. This is the mechanism (already built and used to win clients) that turns Kind-1 contacts into the "qualified conversations" promised in Orange Q2:

  • Step 1 — Study: the AI reads the contact's public posts and profile, and drafts a first message that answers the exact problem they posted about ("your cost per lead is suffering — here's how we bring it down"), not a generic pitch.
  • Step 2 — Loop: it checks back for replies and knows how to send a second and third touch without being pushy.
  • Step 3 — Book: when interest shows, it goes for the booked call — routed through the CRM (text/email sequences), or handed to a human closer for the live conversation.
  • The two levers that decide the rate: who the message appears to come from (a trustworthy profile) and how precisely it mirrors their stated problem. Both are controllable, which is why the 1-in-4 contract rate in Orange Q2 is conservative.
